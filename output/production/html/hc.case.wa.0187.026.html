The testimony as taken before Immigration Inspector Turner, and returned to the Department s hows that Marie Robina, was born at Tridon Bretagne, France, twenty-nine years ago, and that she came to t he United States on the Steamship La Champagne, eleven years ago, and has ever since remained in the United States.

The testimony further s hows that up to the time of her arrival in the United States Marie Robina was not an immoral woman. That after her arrival in the United States she married, in April of her marriage certificate, and thereafter she lived and cohabited with her husband for the succeeding seven month.

The testimony shows that some nine years ago Robina became an immoral woman, and that was over two years after her arrival in the United States, and that since that time she has pursued intermittently the vocation of a sporting woman.

The testimony fur ther shows that the husband, whom she married, William Grayson, was a native born citizen of the United States, born in the City of St. Louis, Missouri, and the record of birth introduced in evidence is returned by the Immigration Inspector.

As we understand t he Governments claim, it is under Section three of the Act of February 20, 1907, as amended by the act of March 26, 1910, and we quote t herefrom, " Any alien who shall be found an inmate of, or connected with the management of a house of prostitution, or practising prostitution after such alien shall have entered the United States, or who s hall receive, share in, or derive benefit from any part of the earnings of any prostitute or is employed by, in, or in connection with any house of prostitution, or music, or dance hall, or other place of amusement, or resort, habitually frequented by prostitutes, or where prostitutes gather, or who in any way assists, protects, or promises to protect, from arrest any prostitute s hall be deemed to be unlawfully within the United States, and shall be deported in the manner provided by Section 20, and 21 of this Act."

Section 20, referred to in Section 3 provides for deportation at any time within t hree years after the arrival of t he alien in this Country.

Section 21 provides for the deportation of such Alien at any time within three years after landing, and directs such removal by the Steamship Company upon whose boat the alien made his entry into this Country.

It will be noted that Section three provides for the deportation of an alien.

If the person sought to be deported is not an alien, then the Statute has no application to such particular person.

One may effect an entry into t his country as an alien and thereafter the status of such personmay be c hanged in various ways.

If such status is c hanged before such person is taken into custody under t his Act, the t he Act does not apply, and the penalty denounced, to-wit, - deportation, cannot be enforced.

One of the methods of a change of status is in the case of a female, by marriage.

The respondent here prior to her entry into the Country was not a prostitute.

Two years ago, being an alien s he married an American Citizen. -1-   For everyone born within t he territorial limits of t he United States is presumed to be a citizen, and ipso facto such marriage Marie Robina took the citizenship of her husband, and thereafter became and ever since has been and now is an American Citizen.

As to the fact of William Grayson's citizenship, there seems to be no controversy.

He was born within this country; if thereafter he expatriated himself it is a matter to be proved.

A given condition is presumed to continue until a change be shown. Citizenship having been once s hown to exist is presumed to continue and there has been no countervailing testimony offered, so that we start with the fact that William Grayson, the husband of the respondent was and is an American Citizen.

The fact of intermarriage between the respondent and William Grayson is conclusively shown.

Then remains the question, what effect does that have upon the status of the wife, admittedly an alien at the time of marriage, but marrying in the United States, a citizen of the United States.

Respondents claim is t hat from the earliest times a woman took the political status of her husband.

Her prior political condition, whatever it might be, became merged by marriage in that of her husband, and whether s he was in fact, at the time of her marriage, a citizen of a foreign Country, by the fact of such marriage her former citizenship was obliterated, and she became a citizen of the Country of w hich her husband was a citizen.

Historical examples of t his are found in the customs of the Teutonic Tribes, when they made raids, for t he purpose of procuring wives, upon other tribes, and being successful, the captured wife took the status of her husband, and thereupon became a member of his tribe, and her allegiance to her native tribe was transferred to the tribe of her husband.

So it was under t he Jewish law.

So it was notably in the case of t he Sabinewomen, where, as it will be remembered the Romans being in need of wives sallied out and captured a number of Sabine women, and took them to wife, and thereupon t hose women, bore and theretofore having lived as Sabines became thereafter Roman matrons, with all the rights and privileges of Roman citizens, and solely by virtue of the marriage relation, and the political status of their husbands.

This Country derives its laws from England.

The common law of England is, at least as far as Federal jurisdiction goes, the law of this country.

Will it be doubted for a moment that under the English common law as it existed prior to the time of George III, and w hich common law became an integral part of the law of this Country, at least within the Federal jurisdiction, that a French woman, marrying an English subject thereby lost her French nationality and acquired a British nationality.

The reason for such rule, which existed before the dawn of written history, existed down through the dark ages, and persisted through the continuance of the common law in England, was this, a wifes' being became merged in t hat of the husband.

The husband, as the bible expresses it, was the head of the wife. There could be but one head.

The wifes' entire being, political and in all other respects, became that of the husband, by marriage.

Her former name became obliterated, her property rights, if she had any, passed ot her husband, her political status was obliterated, because where the husband is the head there could not be in the same head at the same time a condition of two different allegiances The allegiance of the male British subject was due to the British Crown.

The allegiance of the female French citizen was due in those times to the French crown.

By the fact of marriage the male and female became one in law. The citizenship of one thereafter must be obliterated, and the common law, basing its rule upon the biblical rule, and for reasons which commend themselves to any enlightened person, decreed that the citizenship of the female was obliterated, and she took the citizenship of her husband.

And there have been numerous holdings.

In Regina vs. Manning, 2nd Carr. & Kir. 886 The English courts held that a Swiss woman, married to an English subject was not entitled to be tried by a jury of aliens, because she was not an alien, taking the citizenship of her husband.

And in t hat case Chief Baron Pollock observes, -"The obvious, plain and natural inference **** appears to me to be that she should be considered exactly as if s he had been naturalized by the act of Parliament, or as if she had been a natural born subject." Such will be conceded to have been the general rule in English jurisprudence.

(The United States has also legislated uopn t his subject, and by the act of February 10, 1855, it is provided, -"Any woman who is now, or may hereinafter be married to a citizen of the United States, and might herself be lawfully naturalized, s hall be deemed a citizen."

Mr. Bishop in his treaties on the Laws of married women, 2 Bishop, Section 505, says,-"That by t he very act of marriage, citizenship is conferred on a woman, who by previous laws was capable of becoming naturalized. His citizenship conferred citizenship on her.")

And to the same effect is the holding of the Supreme Court of North Carolina, in Kane vs. McCarthy, 63 N.C. 299.

In the case of Leonard vs. Grant, 5 Fed. Rep. page 11, Judge Deady discussing this precise question says, referring to some of the authorities that we have quoted before,-"While the language in all of them is only consistent with a citizenship as enduring, and unqualified as if the wife had been actually naturalized upon her own formal application by the judgment of a competent Court."

The question might then possible arise as to what the meaning of the language is, "and might herself be lawfully naturalized ", and upon that question we have the authority of Judge Deady, a Judge of recognized ability, and in citing from his opinion, in the case last spoken of, it should be borne in mind that at t he time of the rendition of his opinion and the present time, (the requirements of an alien as to naturalization were the same, to-wit, the applicant must be a free white person, or a person of African descent, or nativity.

The applicant must have resided within the United States five years, must have a good moral character, attached to the principles of the Constitution of the United States, and well disposed to the good order and happiness of the same, and must have renounced all titles or orders of nobility, if any applicant had.)

Quoting from Leonard vs. Grant, in Kelly vs. Owen, 7 Wallace, 496, "the question did not appear to have been discussed, or considered but it was assumed that race was the only one of t hese qualifications that it was necessary for the woman to possess at the tim e of her marriage.

In other words, that as the law then stood, she "should be a free white person," and not an alien enemy.

(The reasonable inference is that notwithstanding the law of the Statute, "Might herself be lawfully naturalized", the Supreme Court considered that it was only necessary that the woman should be a person of the class or race permitted to be naturalized by existing laws, and that in respect to the qualifications arising out of her conduct, or opinions, being the wife of a citizen she is to be regarded as qualified for citizenship, and therefore considered a citizen."

Kelly vs. Owen et al, 7 Wallace 496, Co-Operative Edition page 248, Book 19 the Supreme Court of the United States, speaking through Mr. Justice Field, say, "As we construe this act, (that is the act of1855) it confers the privilege of citizenship upon women married to citizens of the United States, if they are of the class of person for whose naturalization the previous acts of Congress provides.)

The terms, "married", or "who shall be married" do not refer in our judgment to the time when the ceremony of marriage, to a citizen, whether his citizenship existed at the passage of the act, or subsequently , or before or after the marriage, she becomes by that fact a citizen also.

His citizenship when ever it exists confers under the act, citizenship upon her.*** The terms "Who might lawfully be naturalized under the existing laws" only limits the application of the law to free white women."

We have here then on this record the case of a women, who lawfully entered the Country, who at the time of entry and long thereafter was a law abiding moral woman, who thereafter married in this Country a citizen of this Country, and who at the time of marriage, for some time before, and for a considerable time there after remained a moral woman.

If she is to be deported it must be upon the ground that she unlawfully entered the Country, is an alien, and is practising prostitution.

It seems to us from the authorities cited that there can be no question but that her entry into the Country was lawful, that thereafter while living in this Country, in a law abiding manner, she married a citizen of this Country, and thereby she became a citizen of the United States, and of course that condition is presumed to continue.

The deportation statute applies only to aliens, it does not apply to citizens of this Country.

If citizens of this Country commit any breach of the laws of the Country, there is a tribunal provided for their trial, where they have a right to be heard in the manner provided by the Constitution before a Court composed of a Judge and a jury of their peers.

The Immigration acts are at best drastic, and it would appear that in order to bring one within such acts the showing must be clear and convincing, both of fact and of law.
