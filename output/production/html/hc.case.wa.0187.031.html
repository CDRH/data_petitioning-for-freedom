RUDKIN, District Judge. On the 24th day of July, 1913, the Acting Secretary of Labor issued a deportation warrant reciting that the petitioner, who landed at the port of New York on the 16th day of December, 1911, has been found in the United States in Violation of the Act of Congress approved February 20, 1907, as amended by the Act approved March 26, 1910, in that the petitioner is a prostitute and has been found practising Prostitution subsequent to her entry into the United States. The petitioner thereupon made application to this court for a writ of habeas corpus on the grounds: first, that she was denied a fair Hearing before the officers of the Department of Labor; second, that she is a Citizen of the United States and the Department was without Jurisdiction to Order her deportation; and, third, that it appears from the testimony taken before the Department that the petitioner is a Citizen of the United States by reason of her marriage to a naturalized Citizen. In support of the petition for the writ proof was offered tending to show that the petitioner and one Benedetto Larcher intermarried in the State of Wyoming on the 26th day of February, 1912, and that the husband, Larcher, was then a naturalized Citizen of the United States. The court was of opinion on the recard before it that the petitioner was accorded a fair hearing before the -1-   officers of the Department of Labor, and that it could not review the order of the Department directing her deportatlon in view of the undertainty of the testimony as to the citizenship of the husband. The proof of marriage and of the citizenship of the husband was therefore rejected, the writ quashed, and the prisoner remanded to the custody of the immigration officer. Thereafter upon application of the Petitioner the Secretary of Labor reopened the case for the purpose of admitting proof of the marriage of the Petitioner and of the citizenship of her husband, but later adhered to its former order of deportation for the reasons stated in the following letter:

"The Department is in receipt of your letter dated December 23rd, No. 425068, transmitting brief of Attorney Fred Miller on behalf of Violet Fadanelli, alias Florence Vogel, alias Violet Larcher, together with naturalization certificate No. 42466, issued at Carlinville, Illinois, to Benedetto Larcher and marriage certificate dated February 26, 1912, of Benedetto Larcher and Violet Fenanly."

"These documents apparently substantiate the testimony given by Violet Fadanelli July 9, 1913, that while she was a prostitute at Diamondville 'A man came to me and said let’s get married and I thought he was just fooling and said alright and we went to Merkener, Wyoming, and got married.’ It is also noted that in reply to the question 'How long did you live with your husband after your marriage?' she stated. ’We went right back to Diamondville and the third day after I got married I was ordered out by the police. I went to Butte, Montana, and haven’t seen my husband since.'"

"The Attorney General has held that where no binding contract of marriage exists and the proceedings are in law a nullity, it is clearly my duty to disregard the ceremony and treat the woman as an allen. In the present oase I find no Intention on the part of the constracting parties to assume the rights and duties incident to the marriage relation, and, further, Violet Fadanelli was not herseif eligible to be lawfully naturalized on account of her immorality."

"The case is therefore closed, and deportation may be proceeded with when the appeal from the findings of Judge Rudkin has been decided."

"Please fumish Attorney Miller with a copy of this letter. The Marriage Certificate and Certificate of Naturalisation enclosed by you are herewith returned in accordance with your request."

I fully agree with the Attorney General that if there was no binding contract of marriage it is the duty of the Department of Labor to disregard matters of form and to treat the woman as an allen. But no such case is presented here. Surely it can not be said that a contract of marriage, solemnized   with all the formalities required by the local law, between parties competent to contract, is a nullity and can be disregarded by the parties or by government officers simply because the woman thought the man was fooling when he asked her to enter into the marriage contract, or because the parties only lived together for a period of three days after the marriage. Every presumption is in favor of the validity of marriage and public policy forbids that the obligation assumed should be thus lightly regarded.

"The statutes now commonly designate the persons who shall have authority to celebrate marriages, including civil magistrates as well as ecclesiastics, at the same time providing that no particular form of ceremony shall be necessary excepting the declaration by the parties that they take each other for husband and wife. A marriage solemnized in full conformity with the statutes is complete without cohabitation, and its validity is not affected by a secret intention or reservation by one of the parties, unknown to the other, nor by their acquiescence in the making and recording of a fraudulent certificate of marriage, which falsely states the date or other particulars of the ceremony.”

26 Cyc., 841. Hilton v. Roylance, 25 Utah, 129, and cases there cited.

Nor can I agree with the conclusion that the petitioner did not become a Citizen upon her marriage under the provisions of Section 1994 of the Revised Statutes because of her immoral character. In an opinion filed In Re Guignon, September 29, 1913, I held that the marriage of an allen woman falling with the general class described in Section 2169, to an American Citizen, conferred citizenship upon the wife even though she did not have or possess the qualifications of residence, good character and other requirements of the naturalization laws, The conclusion there reached is fully supported by the authorities and is recognized by the Supreme Court in Low Wah Suey v. Backus, 225 U. S., 460, 473. The decision in that case was based upon the ground that the woman was a Chinese person not born in the United States and could not therefore be naturalized under the laws of the United States, and not upon her immoral character. The adoption of a different rule would leave the question of the   citizenship of an allen woman married to an American Citizen in a perpetual state of doubt and uncertainty. If she invoked the jurisdiction of the federal court by reason of her alienage she might be defeated on the ground that she was an American Citizen, and if she invoked the like Jurisdiction on the ground of Citizenship she might be defeated by reason of her alienage. She might be permitted to vote at one election and denied the right at the next election because of the nature of the proof offered as to her moral character or as to the view different officers might take of the same proof. Manifestly no such ccohdition was contemplated by Congress. I am therefore of opinion that under the admitted facts in this case the petitioner is an American Citizen and the Department was without Jurisdiction to Order her deportation.

Gonzales v. Williams, 192 U. 3., 1.

The petitioner is therefore unlawfully deprived of her liberty and is entitled to her discharge. Let an Order be entered accordingly.
