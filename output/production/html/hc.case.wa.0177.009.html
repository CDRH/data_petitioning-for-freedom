EXHIBIT F.

UNITED STATES DISTRICT COURT WESTERN DISTRICT OF WASHINGTON. NORTHERN DIVISION.

In the Matter of the Application of Tonka Bajlo for the Writ of Habeas Corpus.

2459 No. 1328

AFFIDAVIT OF FRANK MILLOWAN.

STATE OF WASHINGTON COUNTY OF KING ) ss.

Frank Millowan being first duly sworn on oath deposes and says; that I reside at 623 King Street, Seattle, Washington; that by occupation I am an insurance collector and work for the Great Eastern Casualty Company with offices in the Globe Building on First Avenue in Seattle, Washington, and have resided in the City of Seattle about a year; that I am an Austrian by birth and speak Austrian, Russian, Polish, Hungarian and English languages; that I solicit insurance among people of those nations as well as American people; that I have know Tonka Bajlo about two months; that I first met her while soliciting insurance at 815 Charles Street, Seattle, Washington; that ever since I have known her she was working as cook and housekeeper at 815 Charles Street, wherein boarded some half dozen Austrian laborers; that I know John Skorich who lives at 815 Charles Street and that I solicited him for insurance; that I know a man by the name of Paska Bilin, who resides at the same place, and I became acquainted with some three or four other Austrians who lived there through soliciting them for insurance and became very well acquainted with them. That all of them stated to me during my convervations with them when I would be visiting the place, that the woman who was there whose name was Tonka Bajlo, was working as a cook and keeping house; that I never heard anything except that she was a good, hard working   woman and never heard anyting detrimental to her character. I heard several of them state that her husband was cruel to her and that she was unable to live with him because of his cruelty, and that she was sickly and in poor health because of the cruelty which her husband had inflicted upon her, and that she refused to live with her husband because he had been very cruel to her and continually beat her and subjected her to all kinds of cruelties; that I called at 814 Charles Street many times and became acquainted with all of the boarders, and acquainted with people in the immediate neighborhood, and if Tonka Bajlo had been a woman of disreputable character and guilty of the charge of prostitution which was made against her, I would have had an opportunity to know of it; that the house which was numbered 815 Charles Street, consists of three rooms and a kitchen; that the room which Tonka Bajlo occupied was what was known as the front room facing the street, but there was no outside door to it, and the room next to her was occupied by John Skorich and another man and there was an outside entrance to this room; that the third bedroom was occupied by three or four men, and next to the kitchen; that the kitchen has an outside door entrance.

That I travel around many railroad camps and construction camps among the districts of laborers of foreign nations of this country, and this is not an unusual condition to find and is very common, and it is customary for a man or wife or single woman to engage to keep a boarding camp for laborers who work on the streets, railroads and construction camps; that there is no stigma attached to any woman who engages in such an occupation and lives in the same way as Tonka Bajlo, when a warrant was issued for her arrest upon the charge of being an alien, guilty of prostitution; that on or about the 2nd day of April, 1913, I was called upon to act as an interpreter for Tonka Bajlo at her residence at 815 Charles Street.

Upon the occasion of a visit of her husband to that place when she called upon the police for protection, her husband at that time having made threats that he would kill her, and she had locked herself up in a room by herself when I arrived; that previous to this occasion I had seen letters written by said Jack Bajlo, her husband, in which he threatened to kill Tonka Bajlo and all of the boarders at 815 Charles Street; that after the police had threatened to arrest him, he begged that she let him go free from arrest and stated that he would not bother her any more; that Tonka Bajlo told her husband at that time that she would not live with him; that she had left him because he had been cruel to her and beat her, and that she would not live with him as his wife as long as she remained alive.

That from my general knowledge of the reputation of Tonka Bajlo and from expressions I have heard from those who knew her, I believe her to be a good woman of virtuous character and industrious habits. I never heard anything ill of her previous to the charge made by the Government that she was practicing prostitution.

Frank Millowan.

Subscribed and sworn to before me this 15th day of April, 1913.

Chas. H. Ennis.
Notary Public in and for the State of Washington, residing at Seattle.
