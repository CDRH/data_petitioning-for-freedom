The petitioner now at the earliest opportunity of calls the attention of the Court to points made and authorities citied in Memorandum brief of respondent.

Under title "Facts in the record" counsel refers to one or two lone questions and answers, and stops. We would respectfully refer your Honor to the entire record and particularly that part thereof taken before the Immigrant Inspector at Everett, Washington, bearing date February 6th 1914 which in our opinion covers the principal facts for the consideration of this Court.

Referring to respondent's brief, that portion entitled "Meaning of statute" we disagree with counsel as to his claim that the statute in force at the time of the opinion in Kelly vs Owen (Kahoe) was different from that in force now. The statute has always required that the applicant be a free white person or of African descent or nativity, have resided in the United States for five years, must have good moral character, be attached to the principles of the Constitution of the United States, &c. True the moral character requirement does not appear to have been discussed in Kelly vs.Owen (Kahoe), but such requirement was nevertheless included in the statute then in force and in all naturalization statutes down to the present time.

Careful examination of United States vs. Williams 132 Fed. page 894, discloses the line distinguishing it from the case -1-   at bar and from the cases cited by petitioner. United States vs. Williams was an Immigration case where two minor children of a former alien attempted to procure entrance into this country. At the time of the arrival of the minors at Ellis Island their father had only taken out his first papers. They were both afflicted with trachoma; the Board of enquiry directed their deportation; six days after such order of deportation the father took out his final papers, applied for and obtained a rehearing of the minors' case. The board differed, an appeal was taken to the Department where both minors were ordered excluded. Thereupon a Writ of Habeas Corpus was issued by Judge Lacombe. The judge bases his conclusion clearly and unequivocally upon the ground that the minors were born without the limits of the United States before their father became a citizen. Therefore they were aliens before they arrived at Ellis Island. The father's naturalization did not affect their status, for congress had limited such naturalization in that respect by this language, "The children of persons who have been duly naturalized under any laws of the United States being under the age of twenty-one at the time of naturalization of their parents, shall, if dwelling in the United States, be considered as citizens thereof." These minors never dwelt within the United States; their arrival at Ellis Island was not an entry into this country, hence the conditions prescribed by congress never existed. Petitioner does not in any way question the correctness of the rule laid down in that case, but it has no possible bearing upon the one before our Honor. Here the petitioner concedely entered the country lawfully; thereafter remained within the country lawfully for twelve years; while lawfully in the country concedely married a native born American citizen. The rule is entirely different in exclusion cases from that in deportation cases. In the one the applicant for -2-   admission must show the existence of every qualification prescribed by law; in the other the government assumes the burden of showing that the person sought to be deported is upon some statutory ground not entitled to remain within the country which she has entered, and the reason for the distinction is so plain it is unecessary for us to enlarge upon it. We reiterate that the citizenship of this petitioner became absolutely fixed as of the date of her marriage to Grayson, and the status acquired by marriage can only be changed by a direct proceeding to dissolve the marriage relation.

Another case cited by counsel is United States Vs. Rogers, 185 Federal 334. The distinction between this case and the present case is substantially the same distinction that exists between the case of U.S. vs. Williams and present case. United States vs. Rodgers is an exclusion case and differs in principles as the Court will observe by a reading of the opinion of Judge Gray. The person sought to be excluded in the Rodgers case never succeeded in entering this country, hence acquired no rights, and so the difference in principle and result.

Another case cited by counsel is Re Rustigian 165 Fed. 980 is a naturalization case of final hearing. Upon that final hearing Judge Brown continued the matter and we have no record of the final result; apparently the petitioner was not granted his final papers for the reason, that if granted, his wife (who was then in the custody of the Immigration Officer at New York never having entered the country and suffering from trachoma) might thereby become entitled to admission to this country. The opinion discusses an apparent conflict between the Immigration and Naturalization Laws the sole question in that case to be passed upon was whether the petitioner was entitled to be naturalized; discussion of such question is a discussion of a matter of law involved in a pending proceeding, discussion of anything else is mere obiter and is not authority. It is -3-   respectfully submitted that a large portion of the opinion of Judge Brown was not germane to the question before him, and while entitled to such weight as is the opinion of any lawyer, is entitled to none as the opinion of a judge who may in a cause and on a question depending before him. We however ask the court to read the entire opinion of Judge Brown in this case for it will be observed that the question discussed by Judge Brown was as to the effect of the naturalization of an alien upon that alien's wife who had never entered the country,and as to whether or not ipso facto the naturalization of the husband the non-resident alien wife gained the husband's political status. It is not necessary for us in this case to go to that extent or to make that claim which is far beyond any claim made by petitioner necessary to the decision of this cause; and yet counsel for petitioner and for United States in the Rustigian case agreed that the naturalization of the husband would entitle to entry into this country his non-resident alien wife who was suffering from trachoma.

Counsel cites the opinion of your Honor in re Moola Singh 207 Fed. 780. This was an exclusion case; petitioners never gained an entrance into this country and this court held that the finding of the Immigration Officer was conclusive. We have no occasion to question the correctness of the rule in that opinion. There was no claim in that case that petitioners were American citizens, but on the contrary they were aliens seeking admission into this country. In such cases we concede that the holding of the Department, based upon a fair hearing is conclusive; but not so in the case of one having lawfully entered the country, remained therein lawfully for many years and during such lawful residence having married an American citizen; under those circumstances the holding of the courts is uniform that where the rights of an American citizen are involved and where there is no controversy as to the facts as in this -4-   case, the courts will determine the question of law without regard to any Departmental ruling, and such is the language of Judge Hand in United States vs. Williams 173 Fed. 626 "Where allegiance depends wholly upon a question of law the court must review that question and decide upon it." Nothing need be said here as to the eminent ability of Judge Hand, and this opinion was affirmed by the Circuit Court of Appeals for the 2nd circuit in a unanimous opinion, 184 Fed. 322.

Something was said as to De Bruler vs. Gallo 184 Fed. 566; the opinion in that case proceeds upon the theory that the facts were in dispute and the petitioner had not shown to the satisfaction of the appellate court the existence of American citizenship, and that the decision of the Department as to the facts would not therefore be reviewed by the courts. The court uses this language "Mistakes of law by executive officers are of cource re-examinable by the courts". The court further say "but mere questions of facts such as the case before us where they have been fairly examined by the executive officers designated by congress for that purpose and their conclusions of fact reached upon, conflicting evidence will not be judicially reviewd"; we assent in every way to this statement of the law. it is unquestioned; but that is not this case; here there is no conflict upon the evidence, it is here simply a question of applying the law to the conceded facts, and the claim is that a mistake of law was made by the Department. If there was it is the duty of this court to review and correct that mistake.

The holding of our own Circuit Court of Appeals in Hopkins vs. Fachant, 130 Fed. 839 has never been modified, distinguished or reversed. It is still the settled law throughout the 9th circuit. Law as announced in that case undoubtedly formed the basis for the opinion in re Larcher so that we have the doctrine announced by the highest court in this circuit in harmony with the holdings in other circuits, in holdings harmony -5-   with the decisions of the Supreme Court of the United States, and followed and applied by a distinguished District Judge of this Circuit, and not in any way conflicting with the prior holdings of your Honor in re Moola Singh. Can the rights of an American citizen, rights which have been jealously guarded for a century past be taken away by mere executive order? An American citizen is entitled to her day in court to have her rights determined under the Constitution by the judiciary and not by an executive department.

These remarks are submitted for the reason that petitioner's Attorneys were not in possession of the return until a few moments before the argument, nor had they been served with any points and authorities on behalf of respondent.
