DEFENSE.

Mrs. ALICE McKAY, Petitioner, . after being duly sworn, testified in her own behalf as follows:

Q. [Ronald] Where do you live? A. In Tacoma, at present.

Q. How long have you lived there? A. I have lived about eighteen years there.

Q. Do you know this little girl, Mary McKay? A. That is my child.

Q. Did you leave her on the door step of the Hospital or any other place? A. No sir.

Q. Did you put her in the hospital? A. I took her and left her in the hospital, as I was recovering very slowly myself.

Q. When was that. A. Perhaps a year before this other law suit was had.

Q. About four years ago? A. About that time.

Q. State the circumstances of your taking her to the hospital and leaving her there? A. Me and that young lady there and Mary and Georgie was down with scarlet feaver.

Q. Who do you mean by Georgie? A. A little boy that was carried in there and taken to Vancouver, and I never seen any trace of since.

Q. Who carried him to Vancouver, the sisters? A. Went to their school there.

Q. You were all down with fever? A. I was recovering, they found it out and came down and looked after me. I laid on the floor in Mr. Brown's old building.

Q. Tell what you did? A. hen I was getting well they put me in the hospital and that little girl.

Q. Little Mary? A. Yes ssir

Q. What did you do then? A. I left Mary in the hospital.

Q. Why did you not stany in the hospital? A. They would not let me any more. With these other children.

Q. They would not let these other children be in with you? A. No sir. This girl was a little bigger than Mary is now.

Q. What did you do then? A. I went back into thes old building and lived there.

Q. Did you take any steps --- was Mary still with you? A. Mary was there after that.

Q. What did you do toward doctoring her? A. They were furnished with medicine while she was bad. Dr. Weed waited on her and Dr. Smith too.

Q. Did you visit Mary frequently at the hospital? A. Every two or three days.

Q. Did you any talk with the sisters about her? A. Yes, I visited her. The sisters sat up with her night and day.

Q. She was sick there? A. Yes very bad.

Q. How long did you leave her in the hospital? A. In about two weeks after Mary went to the hospital my husband wrote to me from Port Townsend and wanted me to come to Townsend. That was about six or eight days, and the little girl had gone to this lady's in Belltown.

Q. To Mrs. Keevin's? A. Yes

Q. What did they say? A. The Sister told her she sent her there to get country air to get strong.

Q. What did you do then? A. I went to see Mary on the next day.

Q. Dd you see her? A. I did sir.

Q. What happened then? A. I stood outside of the door and talked to the child and Mrs. Keevin showed me a hat she had got.

Q. What did you say then? A. She said she took a fancy for the child and she said she would liek to get the papers.

Q. What did you say? A. I said no, I could not give any papers on that child.

Q. Did you promise to bring papers in a week or any time? A. No sir. The sisters have frequently asked for papers, I never promised to give papers on my child.

Q. They frequently asked for papers? A. Yes, the Sisters any time I went to see them they wanted fpapers on my child. Up to the time that girls leg was broken and I never went near that sister again.

Q. Mrs. Keevin asked if she could have papers? A. Yes, she asked me for papers.

Q. What did you tell her? A. I told her no.

Q. How frequently have you been back there? A. I have gone frequently to see the child, made several trips. I would be generally left outside of the door and the child would be brought in, I might just see her, I was never allowed to kiss the child, they would give her a crack in the back of the head and send her back in the room.

Q. Who done that? A. Mrs. Keevin, she said she did not want me to come, that she did not want Mary to know I was her mother.

Q. Do you like Mary? A. I lowe that child, I love her in my heart, that is my child and I want her.

Q. How often have you tried to get her, asked for her. A. At the time her leg was broken I had a suit about her, and then I took out a writ of habeas corpus, Judge [Mrs. McKay, D-EX. [] 76--147. 4   Greene denied the writ on account of my extreme poverty, at that time.

A. Yes sir.

Q. Are you able to cloathe her warmly A. Yes sir.

Q. School her? A. Yes sir.

Q. Did you ever go to Mrs. Keevens house drunk? A. No sir.

Q. Never went there drunk? A. No sir

Q. Now Mrs. McKay, do you live in the house at Tacoma with Antoine anybody?

A. I dont live w ith any man sir, never lived with any man outside of my husband.
