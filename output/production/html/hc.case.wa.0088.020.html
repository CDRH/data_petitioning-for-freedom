C. H. HANFORD, a witness called on behalf of the Respondent, after being duly sworn, testified as follows:

Q. [Gilman] Mr. Hanford, do you know the petitioner in this case, Alice McKay? A. Yes sir.

Q. How long have you known her? A. Something like six or seven years.

Q. Do you know what her character is as a law abiding woman and as a temparate woman?

RONALD: We object to that.

The COURT: Do you mean her public reputation?

GILMAN: I intended to prove both.. I do not know that I can prove any specific acts by Mr. Hanford.

The COURT: You ask then simply if he knows her general reputation as a law abiding citizen.

Q. Do you know what her general reputation is as a law abiding woman and as a temperate woman?

Mr. RONALD: At the present time?

The COURT: It is in the present tense that the question is asked.

A. Well, just at the present time I do not think I know much about her reputation, within the last year I have not known much about her or her reputation.

Q. Do you know what it was prior to that time?

Mr. RONALD: We object.

A. Yes.

Q. What was it?

Mr. Ronald: We except.

A. I cannot say I know as to temperance, but as to her being a law breaker I know her reputation.

Q. What was it? A. It is bad. Persistent, troublesome violater of the law.

Q. In 1883-4 you were assistant district attorney were you not? A. Yes sir.

Q. Did you ever have occasion to prosecute this woman? A. I did.

Q. When? A. It was in the fall of 1883 at Tacoma.

Q. For what offense?

RONALD: We object it is immaterial.

The COURT: Objection overruled and exception noted.

A. For selling spiritous liquors to Indians.

Q. With what result?

RONALD: We object.
