The first error alleged occurred in selecting a jury. The person was challenged for cause on the ground that he had once already served on a jury as a talesman in the trial of a cause at the same term of the court, the court overruled the challenge. This was error. Code sec 270. It appears that after the person had served as a talesman he had ben called to serve as a regular juror for the term in place of a juror excused. This made no difference, the law remained the same, the challenge should have been allowed, the beneficent objects of the section could not be frustrated by placing a man on the regular panel who could not serve on a jury if challenged. The [?] object of the law is to get rid of professional jurors, a most pertiferous set as every lawyer knows, and this could not be done away with by a sheriff or court, by making and putting such a jurer on the regular panel[?]. The juror was afterwards challenged peremptorily by plantiff in then[?] and thus removed from the jury. The record does not show that the defendant exhausted his peremptory challenges. Then facts bring the case directly within the ruling of this court in the case of Merton v The State 1st [?] 468, "By this means the defendant seems   2 effectually to have relieved himself from the grievance of which he complains" and the case will not be reserved for an error of the court which the party caused by his own action.

The next error claimed is that the court used in the charge to the jury and special attention is called to the 6th instruction which is as follows

"That the letter of instructions of commisioner Mix, does not justify the illegal act of Agent Wiley. He is answerable in damages to the plaintiff for any illegal act."

The plaintiff in error claims that by the instruction the court total the jury that Wiley did do some illegal act to Keokuk and that such act was illegal and that the letter of commisioner Mix did not justify such illegal act. It is apparent from the record that the court did do just what it is claimed that it did, the indication of the court is too plain not to have been understood by the jury, did the pleadings and evidence authorize such an instruction. The jury are only to pass upon the issues made up in the case. They pass upon the controverted facts, that is the facts controverted by the proceedings. In this case the petition alleged an assault and battery and false imprisonment . The answer set up two grounds of defense

  3

first a general denial and second a special defense showing what the defendant had really done and attempting to justify his acts by these facts. It was an admission that the defendant was the primary moving cause of the arrest and imprisonment of the plaintiff and an attempt to justify his conduct therein. Now notwithstanding the general denial we suppose that whatever was admitted in the special defense need not have been proved. This special defense so far modified the general denial that whatever was admitted therein was excepted from the general denial. It ought to be so on general principle of pleading, it must be so in as much as it is a formal confession of the existence of the facts therein set forth. A careful examination of the answer has convinced us that had there been no evidence in the case the court would have had to give nominal damages at least on the pleadings as they stood, and to do so would necessarily have held that in the answer was an admission of facts that made the defendant guilty of an illegal act in the ane causing the arrest and false imprisonment of Keokuk.

The court there committed no error in telling the jury as it did that Wiley had been guilty of an illegal act towards the plaintiff and of course there can be no justification in law

for an illegal act. If there is justification in law, there can be no illegallity. As the pleadings had admitted the acts done it was proper for the court to set so say to the jury and it was its duty to say to them that such acts were illegal if such was the law.

Nor do we think this ruling is at all in conflict with the case of Carl Horne v The State | Kansas 42. to which we have been refused. The plea of not Guilty in that case put everything in don issue, and it was necessary for the state to prove every fact essential to make up the crime, and every such fact was to be passed upon by the jury, unbiased by the court anything the court might say. In this case it was the duty of the court on the pleadings to say that the judgement must be for the plaintiff and it was proper to say & do to the jury, and the court, did no more at most in the instruction under consideration

The seventh and eighth instructions are objected to They are as follows

7" That whenever the elements of frand malice gross negligence or oppression mingle in the controversy the law allows the jury to give   5 what is called exemplary or vindictive damages"

8 " That in cases of torts and actions for false imprisonment, the jury are the only proper judges of how much the plaintiff ought to recover,"

These instructions raise the question so much discussed of late by writers upon law as to whether such damages as are called exemplary, vindictive or punitive ought ever to be allowed We content ourselves with following the current of authorities and decide that the instructions go no further than such authorities warrant. If the law is wrong let the law making power correct it. The rule as laid down by the court below has already received the sanctions of this court. Malone v Murphy 2nd Kansas 250., The whole subject is discussed pro and con, and the authorities refused to in 2 Greenleafs[?] Ev. secs 253-5 and note, and in Secs.[?] on measure of damages 4th Ed. 533 and note. And after all this discussion, the Supreme Court of the US decide the law as laid down in these instructions. Mr Justice Grier delivering the opinion of the court well says. "If repeated judicial decision for more than a century are to be received as the best experition of what the law is, the question will not admit of argument. By the common as well as by statute laws, men are often punished for aggravated misconduct of lawless acts by means of a civil action, and the damages inflicted by way of penalty or punishment, given to the party

  6

injured" We have no doubt that such is the law. Where it be founded in sound reason or not is not so much our province to say, as to determine if it be law. The writer hereof believes it to be not only good law but founded on sound principles and beneficial in its application. It often furnishes the only restraint upon a bad man who cares little for his neighbors character his person, or his property. The party injured pursues the wrong doer to punishment, when society is too careless to do so.

It is claimed however in this case that the action being for assault and battery, which is punishable by criminal proceedings, if exemplary damages are allowed, then the wrong doer is liable to be punished twice. To this two answers may be made each of which is satisfactory, while the action is for assault and battery and false imprisonment, the evidence only showed a false imprisonment and the plaintiff fails to show how that is made a criminal offence again. the great weight of authorities is against such a modification of the doctrine. In Massachusetts, Indiana and North Carolina such a modification limitation has been engrafted upon the doctrine of exemplary damages, but in most of the states it has been rejected and we think rightfully.

  7

These reasons render it unnecessary to consider at length the instructions asked by the defendant and refused, for they are the direct converse of those given, asking the court to say that the jury could only give a verdict for the damages sustained or "actually sustained" The petition contained the usual averments formerly found in actions for assault and battery and false imprisonment and the jury were authorized under it to give exemplary damages as we understand the law, and the instructions asked laid down a much more limited rule and consequently ought not to have been given

On a motion for a new trial it was insisted[?] that the verdict was against the law and the evidence and two reasons are urged in support of the petition. First that the evidence did not show a state of facts that would authorize a recovery against Wiley, Set as see. Wiley was agent for the Sac and Fox Indians of which tribe Keokuk was chief. Commissioner Mix had directed that no delegations from any of the tribes should visit Washington as there was no appropriation to pay their expenses. This letter as was one from Superintendent Murphy was read by Wylie to Keokuk and others on the 16th day of November 1868. When Keokuk said he had should go as he had money and would pay his own expenses. That on the 22nd of November Keokuk and four others of the tribe started for Washington without license so to do.

That they were overtaken by Wylie at Lawrence on the next day. That Wylie on that day went before a United States Commissioner for the district of Kansas and made a complaint in writing under oath, charging that Keokuk a Sac and Fox Indian of the Sac and Fox reserve in the county of Franklin in the district and state of Kansas did on the 23d day of November 1868 leave said reserve, and did disobey the order of the United States Commissioner of Indian Affairs and the order of other agents of the United States to him given and asking that Keokuk be apprehended and dealt with according to law. On this affidavit a warrant was issued and Keokuk arrested by the United States marshall and brought before the Commissioner, Wylie employed council and certified in the case, and thereupon the commissioner decided that Keokuk should give bail for his appearance at the next term of the United States Court for the district of Kansas, and directed the marshall to take him into custody. Keokuk refused to give bail and was taken to jail and kept there till he was released upon habeas corpus.

This is a sufficient outline of the testimony in the case to understand what is deemed necessary to say on the point under consideration. The complaint made by Wylie charges no offense on the part of Keokuk, neither did   9 the warrant or mittimus, describe any offence known to the law. The substance of the complaint is stated above, and the warrant and Mittimus generally follow it, though the mittimus is more verbose, as though the Commissioner felt it necessary to say something more, than that Keokuk had left the reserve and was travelling to Washington to justify his act in sending him to jail. Still no offence is charged known to any system of law of which we have any knowledge. The facts are so barren as not to lay the foundation for jurisdiction. The affidavit warrant and Mittimus afford ample evidence that no criminal charge was prefered or passed upon, nor a sufficient pretense of one to challenge judicial direction and this conclusion sweeps away the ground on which the decisions rested to which the plaintiff in error refuse in his brief.
