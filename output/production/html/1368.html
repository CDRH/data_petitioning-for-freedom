  • Summary of Proceedings: Jerome E. Corbin filed a writ of habeas corpus to release his son, Frank E. Corbin, a minor who wrongfully represented to recruiters that he was twenty-one when he was only eighteen. Jerome claimed to have sent numerous letters to the War Department in 1903 to request his son's release. Judge Hanford approved the petition and ordered the Army to produce Corbin in a week, by January 18. The writ was served on Colonel J. F. Huston, the officer in charge of Company D, 9th Infantry. In their response, the government -- represented by three attorneys -- claimed that the young man was already subject to a military court proceeding for fraudulent enrollment. Claiming congressional supremacy and citing precedent that in cases involving courts martial, the military has jurisdiction over claims of Habeas Corpus in advance of any other precedings. Jerome Corbin filed a response, claiming that the Army proceedings came after the writ was filed. Judge Hanford agreed with the government's position and returned Corbin to Colonel Huston for the military preceding.
