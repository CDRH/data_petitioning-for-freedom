NO. 2459 FILED May 14, 1913.

CUSHMAN, District Judge.

Petitioner seeks discharge from the custody of the Commissioner of Immigration upon an application for a writ of habeas corpus. In the petition it is alleged that she was arrested upon a warrant issued by the Secretary of Labor, charging her with bein "an alien found practicing prostitution after entry"; that, at the hearing held April 7, 1913, the said Tonka Bajlo was compelled to testify against herself upon a criminal charge; that she was not informed that she had a right to refuse to testify against herself; that she had no opportunity to present evidence for or on her behalf; that, on the evidence produced at said hearing, the Secretary of Labor ordered that she be deported from this country to Austria from whence she came upon a criminal charge, that of practicing prostitution after entry into this country; That she does not understand the English language and did not understand the nature of the proceeding against her, and when informed of the result thereof she demanded that she have counsel and a rehearing; that the order for her deportation has been made by the Honorable Secretary of Labor without an opportunity for a hearing before the Commissioner of Immigration at the Port of Seattle; that she is imprisoned and restrained of her liberty by reason of false charges made and instigated against her by Jack Bajlo, her husband, who is seeking to wreak revenge   upon her because of the fact that she refused to live with him by virtue of his inhuman treatment of her and his constant and continuous brutal acts of cruelty.

The foregoing allegations are denied by the return. It is alleged affirmatively that, upon an affidavit charging petitioner "with being an alien found in the United States in violation of the Act of Congress approved February 20, 1907, and amended by the Act of March 26, 1910, to-wit:- That she was an alien found practicing prostitution after her entry into the United States", a warrant was issued by the Secretary of Labor, upon such evidence, being satisfied that petitioner was subject to deportation under the laws of the United States, ordered her deported to the country from whence she came. A copy of the evidence taken upon the hearing is made a part of the return to the writ.

The jurisdiction of the court to review the acts of the Secretary of Labor is denied. The matter is now submitted to the court upon the sufficiency of the return to the writ.

The warrant upon which petitioner was arrested gave a ground therefor that she was "a prostitute and has been found practicing prostitution subsequent to her entry into the United States". The warrant of deportation, after hearing had, recites, as additional grounds therefor, that petitioner was "a person liable to become a public charge at the time of entry." The Commissioner of Immigration also found from the evidence taken at the hearing that petitioner was "of the excluded classes at the time of entry; i.e., a person who admits having committed an offense involving moral turpitude."

It is argued that petitioner had no hearing because the warrant upon which she was arrested did not contain these two latter grounds. So far as this case is concerned, that may be conceded, for it was found, and the warrant or deportation does recite as a ground therefor, the charge and recital in the warrant of arrest that she was a prostitute and had been found practicing prostitution Therefore, petitioner cannot complain that she was arbitrarily deprived of a fair hearing on the latter charges and ordered deported, unless there was no evidence to support that first charge.

It is claimed that petitioner's husband made the affidavit upon which she was arrested and testified against her before the Immigration officer through spite and jealously; that the admissions and testimony taken upon the hearing, both of petitioner and other witnesses, were misunderstood and misinterpreted. These arguments, themselves, show that a hearing was had and evidence given, that petitioner, herself, admits requires explanation. Even if it were conceded that the   record of the evidence may be attacked by the affidavits of witnesses afterwards, nothing is shown in the way of mistake in the evidence that would justify the conclusion that the order of deportation was controlled by the disputed testimony.

The evidence shows that petitioner had a hearing; that she waived representation by counsel, in effect, because she did not deem that she could afford to employ an attorney, and her petition, itself, shows that she only requested an opportunity to have an attorney after she learned of the decision.

No oppressive nor unfair means appear to have been employed. There was evidence adduced at the hearing sufficient to support the finding and order.

Complaint is made that the Immigration officer included in his report an expression of his opinion, formed from an examination of the house in which petitioner lived with a number of men, that the conditions he found had led him to believe that she must have engaged in the practice of prostitution.

Without deciding the effect of this, in the absence of any hearing on this subject, in the case, it is sufficient to say that petitioner was fully heard concerning this house and her life therein. If there was any explanation to be made, she had an opportunity to make it. The court will not undertake to review the action of the Secretary of Labor on the ground that immaterial or irrelevant evidence was admitted, even if that of which complaint is made is considered to be such. It is sufficient to say that the petitioner was not being tried upon a criminal charge, which is a complete answer to the other grounds set out in her petition.

If it were conceded that the husband of petitioner was acting against her through malice, that is a matter that would affect the weight and credibility of his evidence --- matters solely to be considered by the Secretary and within his discretion --- and not going to its competency. The husband's   sworn testimony must be considered as evidence.

The order to show cause is discharged.
