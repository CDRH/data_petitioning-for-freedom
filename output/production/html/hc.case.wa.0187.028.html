FACTS IN THE RECORD.

It appears that the petitioner was practicing prostitution at the time of her marriage. To sustain this see page 2 of the first hearing, where she said she was a sporting women nine years ago, and on page 3, of the same hearing, that she was practicing prostitution when she met her husband.

To sustain the proposition that she was married while released on bond, pending a hearing before the Immigration authorities, see the files of the Immigration Office.

STATUTE CONTROLLING CASE.

Mr. Shakespeare correctly quotes Section 19994 R.S.,see Vol. 1 Fed. Stat. Annot page 786, which is in the following language:

"Any woman who is now, or may hereafter be married to a citizen of the United States, and who might herself be lawfully naturalized, shall be deemed a citizen."

We agree with Mr. Shakespeare, that Mr. Bishop is correct in giving the law, as follows:

"That by the very act of marriage, citizenship is conferred upon a woman, who by previous laws was capable of becoming naturalized. His citizenship conferred citizenship on her."

 

MEANING OF STATUTE.

In the case of Kelly v. Kahoe, 19 L. Ed. 284, the Supreme Court in construing this Act says:

"It confers the privileges of citizenship upon women married to citizens of the United States, if they are of the class of persons for whose naturalization the previous acts of Congress provide."

And the court further says:

"The terms 'who might lawfully be naturalized under the existing law' only limit the application of the law to free white women. The previous naturalization act existing at the time only required that the person applying for its benefits should be a free white person, and not an alien enemy."

And the Supreme Court cites the naturalization act of April 14, 1802, 2 Stat. L. p. 153.

But the naturalization law in force at the time of the marriage of the petitioner was different from that in force at the time of the decision of the Supreme Court in the Kelly v. Kahoe case, supra and is correctly quoted by Mr. Shakespeare in his brief as being as follows:

"The applicant must be a free white person, or a person of African descent or nativity. The applicant must have resided within the United States five years, must have a good moral character, attached to the principles of the Constitution of the United States, and well disposed to the good order and happiness of the same, and must have renounced all titles or orders of nobility, if any applicant has."

There is no more reason for dispensing with the requirement, that a woman have a good moral character, than there is for dispensing with the requirement, that she be a free white person.
